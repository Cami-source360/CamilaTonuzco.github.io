Project 04

3-DOF RRP SCARA Robot

Development and Control of a Compact SCARA Manipulator for Precision Motion Applications

Project Details

Completed: November 2025

Project Overview

This project focused on the development of a compact 3-degree-of-freedom SCARA robot designed for positioning, trajectory execution, and drawing applications. The manipulator combines mechanical transmission systems, embedded control, and high-level motion planning to achieve smooth and coordinated movement.

The platform was conceived as an experimental robotics system capable of validating concepts related to kinematics, motion control, interpolation, and embedded real-time programming.

Main project capabilities included:

  • Planar motion using an RRP SCARA configuration
  • Vertical end-effector actuation through a linear mechanism
  • Precision-enhanced pulley transmission system
  • Embedded cascade motor control
  • Continuous trajectory generation and execution
  • Real-time visualization and monitoring through ROS 2

Mechanical Design

The mechanical architecture was developed with emphasis on precision, rigidity, and ease of manufacturing. The robot structure consists of a rotating base, two articulated links, and a vertical actuation mechanism mounted on the second arm.

To improve positioning resolution, both rotational joints were coupled through a 4:1 pulley reduction system. Several design iterations were evaluated during development, including a spring-based tensioning mechanism. Experimental testing later showed that a fixed tensioning configuration provided improved stability and repeatability.

Additional structural reinforcement was introduced on the first arm to reduce bending effects during operation. The vertical axis uses a compact rack-and-pinion mechanism that allows controlled movement of the drawing tool.

The entire assembly was modeled in Autodesk Inventor to validate dimensions, transmission alignment, and component integration prior to fabrication.

Most structural components were manufactured using laser-cut acrylic, while PETG 3D-printed parts were used for geometrically complex sections such as the rack-and-pinion assembly. Custom shafts and couplings were machined in steel and brass to improve durability and transmission reliability.

Electronics

The electronic architecture was centered around a custom PCB developed to support both the SCARA manipulator and additional robotics platforms created throughout the course.

The board integrated:

  • Motor driver circuitry
  • Power regulation stages
  • Encoder and sensor interfaces
  • Communication peripherals
  • PWM-based actuator control

The modular design simplified system integration and reduced wiring complexity while enabling rapid testing of different robotic configurations.

Special attention was given to signal reliability and power distribution to ensure stable operation during simultaneous multi-axis motion.

Motion Control Strategy

The robot executes point-to-point motion through inverse kinematic calculations that transform Cartesian target positions into joint-space references.

A synchronized timing strategy coordinates the movement of all joints, allowing the manipulator to transition smoothly between positions while maintaining stable and predictable motion.

This approach enabled reliable positioning performance during both discrete movements and continuous trajectory execution.

Trajectory Planning and Drawing

Beyond simple positioning tasks, the manipulator was designed to execute continuous paths and parametric trajectories generated from engineering drawings.

The software framework supports multiple interpolation methods, including:

  • Linear interpolation
  • Cubic interpolation
  • Quintic interpolation

Trajectory generation can be performed in both task space and joint space, allowing smoother transitions and improved motion quality during drawing operations.

This architecture enabled the robot to reproduce complex geometries while maintaining continuity and minimizing abrupt velocity changes.

Embedded Firmware Architecture

The low-level controller was implemented in object-oriented C++ and executed using hardware-timed loops running independently from the communication middleware.

Dedicated timers handled deterministic execution of the control algorithms, ensuring stable real-time behavior even during communication or visualization tasks.

The robot uses a cascade control structure composed of:

  • An outer-loop proportional position controller
  • An inner-loop discrete PID velocity controller

The firmware continuously processes encoder feedback, estimates joint velocity, computes tracking errors, and generates PWM control signals for motor actuation.

This multi-layer control strategy improved tracking accuracy, responsiveness, and motion smoothness during dynamic trajectory execution.

High-Level Software and ROS 2 Integration

High-level trajectory generation and visualization were implemented in ROS 2 running on a host computer.

A dedicated software node converts DXF drawings into dense point clouds, generates interpolated trajectories, and solves the inverse kinematics of the RRP manipulator.

Additional interpolation is then applied in configuration space to improve motion smoothness before execution.

Main software features included:

  • DXF preprocessing and point extraction
  • Task-space interpolation
  • Inverse kinematic calculations
  • Real-time trajectory streaming
  • RViz-based visualization (digital twin)

The microcontroller continuously transmitted joint-state estimates back to ROS 2, enabling live comparison between desired and measured trajectories.

Experimental Results

Experimental validation demonstrated stable and accurate execution of both linear and curved trajectories.

The manipulator successfully reproduced complex paths while maintaining smooth transitions and coordinated multi-axis motion. The results validated the effectiveness of the mechanical design, embedded control architecture, and trajectory planning framework.
